# Macroscopic Energy Conservation Equation in Heat Transfer

The macroscopic energy conservation equation in heat transfer describes the storage, transfer (thermal conduction $k$, thermal convection $u$, and thermal radiation $r$), and transformation of thermal energy into other forms.

## 1. **Energy Conservation**

$$
\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{q} = -\rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + \sum_{i,j} \dot{s}_{i-j},
$$

$$
\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{q} = \frac{\int_{\Delta A} (\boldsymbol{q} \cdot \boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{n}}) dA}{\Delta V \to 0}.
$$

Here, $\rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}$ represents **sensible heat storage**, and $\dot{s}_{i-j}$ is the energy conversion rate of thermal energy determined by the nature and frequency of interactions between energy carriers $i$ and $j$.

## 2. **Heat Flux Vector**

$$
\boldsymbol{q} = \boldsymbol{q}_k + \boldsymbol{q}_u + \boldsymbol{q}_r
$$

The heat flux vector $\boldsymbol{q}$ is the sum of conduction, convection, and radiation heat flux vectors.

### **Conduction Heat Flux Vector**

$$
\boldsymbol{q}_k = -k \nabla T
$$

The **conduction heat flux vector** $\boldsymbol{q}_k$ is the product of the thermal conductivity $k$ and the negative temperature gradient $\nabla T$, according to Fourier's law of heat conduction.

### **Convection Heat Flux Vector**

$$
\boldsymbol{q}_u = \rho c_p \boldsymbol{u} T
$$

The **convection heat flux vector** $\boldsymbol{q}_u$ is the product of $\rho c_p$, the local velocity vector $\boldsymbol{u}$, and the temperature $T$.

### **Radiation Heat Flux Vector**

$$
\boldsymbol{q}_r = 2\pi \int_0^\infty \int_{-1}^1 \boldsymbol{s} I_{ph,\omega} d\mu d\omega
$$

The **radiation heat flux vector** $\boldsymbol{q}_r$ is the integral of the product of the unit vector $\boldsymbol{s}$ and the directional radiative intensity $I_{ph,\omega}$ across space and the electromagnetic spectrum.

## Major Energy Carriers

Four types of energy carriers—**phonons** ($p$), **electrons** ($e$), **fluid particles** ($f$), and **photons** ($ph$)—form the microscopic model of thermal energy storage, transfer, and interactions.

### 1. **Phonons**

- Phonons are quantized vibrational modes in a rigid atomic lattice.
- Long-wavelength phonons give rise to sound in solids, hence the name "phonons."
- Phonons determine many physical properties of materials, including heat capacity and thermal/electrical conductivity (phonon propagation governs heat conduction in insulators).
- In classical mechanics, any lattice vibration can be decomposed into a superposition of nonlocal normal vibrational modes.
- Quantum mechanically, these modes exhibit particle-like properties (wave-particle duality). Thus, phonons are **quasiparticles**.
- Phonons are **bosons** with zero spin, and their energy $E_p = \hbar \omega_p$ is the sum of potential and kinetic energy, where $\hbar = h/2\pi$ and $h$ is Planck's constant.
- Two types of phonons:
  - **Acoustic phonons** (A): Frequency decreases with increasing wavelength, corresponding to sound waves in the lattice. Subtypes include longitudinal (LA) and transverse (TA) phonons.
  - **Optical phonons** (O): Occur in lattices with multiple atoms per unit cell and are easily excited by light. Subtypes include longitudinal (LO) and transverse (TO) optical phonons.

---

### 2. **Electrons**

- Electrons are negatively charged **subatomic particles** with spin 1/2 (fermions).
- Their antiparticles are **positrons**, which have the same mass and spin but opposite charge.
- Electrons exhibit both particle and wave properties and can be considered **quasiparticles**. Electrons bound to the nucleus manifest as **standing waves**.
- In solids, electrons are classified into:
  - **Core electrons**: Do not participate in bonding and move with the nucleus.
  - **Valence electrons**: Include **conduction electrons** (free electrons) and those in the outer shell.
- Described quantum mechanically by the **Dirac equation**.
- Electron energy comprises potential energy (bond energy) and kinetic energy (dependent on velocity).

---

### 3. **Fluid Particles**

- Gases and liquids consist of atoms or molecules (collectively referred to as fluid particles) in **random motion**.
- Fluid particle energy includes potential, electronic, and kinetic energy.
- For an **ideal gas**, particle collisions are **elastic**, and intermolecular forces are negligible.
- Ideal gas behavior is described by the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, derived using statistical mechanics.

---

### 4. **Photons**

- Photons are quanta of the electromagnetic field and fundamental particles in quantum electrodynamics.
- Photons exhibit wave-like properties and have zero rest mass but finite energy. They are subject to gravity due to their energy, according to general relativity.
- Photons are **bosons** with spin 1 and are circularly polarized.
- Energy of a photon:

$$
E_{ph} = \hbar \omega_{ph},
$$

where $\omega_{ph} = 2\pi f_{ph}$ is the angular frequency, and $f_{ph}$ is the frequency.
- Momentum of a photon:

$$
\boldsymbol{p}_{ph} = \hbar \boldsymbol{\kappa}_{ph},
$$

where $\boldsymbol{\kappa}_{ph}$ is the wave vector.

## Energy Distribution Functions

For systems with multiple particles, the observed **macroscopic state** (ensemble average) is determined by the probability of each microscopic state (energy distribution function) and the **microscopic states** (positions and momenta) of each particle:

$$
\langle \phi \rangle = \sum_i f_i \phi_i.
$$

### Equilibrium Distribution Functions:

1. **Bose-Einstein Distribution** (Phonons, Photons):

$$
f_i^0 = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{E_i}{k_B T}\right) - 1}.
$$

2. **Fermi-Dirac Distribution** (Electrons):

$$
f_i^0 = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{E_i - \mu}{k_B T}\right) + 1}.
$$

3. **Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution** (Ideal Gas Molecules):

$$
f_i^0 = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{E_i}{k_B T}\right)}.
$$

## Contributions to Heat Transfer Physics

Heat, a form of energy manifested as molecular motion, is transferred through **conduction** (phonons, electrons, fluid particles), **convection** (fluid particles), and **radiation** (photons).

### Key Contributions:

- **Boltzmann**: Developed energy equipartition theory and introduced the Boltzmann constant.
- **Maxwell**: Founded electromagnetic theory and contributed to gas kinetics and thermodynamics.
- **Planck**: Discovered quantum energy quantization, proposing that energy is emitted/absorbed in discrete quanta.
- **Schrödinger**: Established quantum wave mechanics and the Schrödinger equation.
- **Green & Kubo**: Developed fluctuation-dissipation theory for transport coefficients.
- **Ziman**: Applied variational principles to phonon transport properties.

## Fundamental Constants and Scales

### Boltzmann Constant:

$$
k_B = 1.38065 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{J/K}.
$$

### Planck Constant:

$$
h = 6.626069 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s}, \quad \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi}.
$$

### Atomic Units:

- Atomic length: 

$$
r_B = \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_e e_c^2} = 5.2918 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}.
$$

- Atomic time: 

$$
\tau_a = \frac{m_e r_B^2}{\hbar} = 2.4189 \times 10^{-17} \, \text{s}.
$$

- Atomic energy: 

$$
\frac{e_c^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r_B} = 4.3597 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J} = 27.211 \, \text{eV}.
$$
